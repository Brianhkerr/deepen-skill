## D1. How much of expert performance does deliberate practice explain?

**The disagreement.** Ericsson's camp: practice is the dominant, near-sufficient driver and apparent
"talent" mostly reflects accumulated practice. Macnamara/Hambrick camp: practice is one important
factor among several, explaining a large *minority* of variance.

**Operationalized.** What fraction of between-person performance variance does accumulated deliberate
practice account for, across domains?

**Crux.** Whether accumulated-practice measures, when corrected for measurement and definition, explain
**more or less than ~50%** of variance. Both sides' conclusions hinge on this number.

**My stance.** Practice explains a **large minority — roughly 20–25% in the practice-friendly domains
(games, music, sports) and much less in ill-structured ones.** Necessary, not sufficient. I side with
the Macnamara meta-analytic estimate over Ericsson's stronger claim.
- **Confidence: HIGH** — because the estimate is meta-analytic (88 studies), the direction is
  reinforced by an independent line of evidence (chess hours-to-mastery spread, twin genetics), and the
  pre-registered 2019 revisit moved *toward* the lower estimate, not away
  ([Macnamara & Maitra, 2019](https://royalsocietypublishing.org/doi/10.1098/rsos.190327)).
- **Falsifier.** A pre-registered, definition-tight study using Ericsson's *narrow* deliberate-practice
  criteria (not broad "practice") that recovers >50% variance explained in a core domain. That would
  show the low estimates are an artifact of mis-measurement, exactly as Ericsson argues.
- **Survived refutation.** Skeptic's best shot: "the meta-analysis used a broad definition that isn't
  Ericsson's construct, so it understates the true effect" — a real, sourced objection
  ([Ericsson 2016 / reply](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/27217248/)). Why it doesn't flip me: the 2019
  revisit *did* isolate teacher-designed practice (closer to the narrow construct) and still found no
  significant best-vs-good difference and a smaller effect than 1993. The definitional objection wounds
  the precise % but not the **necessary-not-sufficient** conclusion. → **Stance kept, confidence held.**

## D2. Is the "10,000-hour rule" a useful heuristic or a harmful myth?

**The disagreement.** Pop-science: a motivating rule of thumb. Researchers: a distortion.

**Crux.** Whether a fixed hour-count meaningfully predicts mastery across people and domains.

**My stance.** **Harmful myth, not a useful heuristic.** The hours-to-mastery spread is roughly an
order of magnitude within a single domain (chess: ~3k to >23k), the figure was a group *average* that
half the top group hadn't reached, and the originating researcher rejects it.
- **Confidence: HIGH** — multiple independent confirmations, including the author's own disavowal.
- **Falsifier.** Evidence that hours-to-criterion is tightly clustered (low variance) within a domain
  *and* stable across domains. The chess data alone falsifies this today.
- **Survived refutation.** Skeptic: "even if literally wrong, it motivates people to practice — net
  positive." Why it doesn't flip me: `[inference]` a false threshold also *demotivates* fast learners who
  expect to grind 10k hours and demoralizes slow ones who pass 10k without mastery; "necessary but not
  sufficient, quality over count" is both truer and at least as motivating. → **Stance kept.**

## D3. Can genetics route *through* practice itself?

**The disagreement.** Standard model: practice is the environmental input, talent the genetic one.
Mosing et al.: the propensity to practice and the benefit from it are themselves partly genetic.

**Crux.** Whether the practice→ability correlation survives genetic control (within identical-twin
pairs).

**My stance.** **Yes — at least partially, the practice→ability link is genetically confounded.** Within
monozygotic pairs, the twin who practiced more was not reliably better
([Mosing et al., 2014](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/25079217/)).
- **Confidence: MEDIUM** — one flagship design, one domain (music), and a counter-intuitive result that
  deserves replication before I treat the *magnitude* as settled. The *direction* (some genetic
  confounding exists) I hold more firmly than the size.
- **Falsifier.** Genetically-informative or intervention studies showing a clear within-pair (or
  randomized) causal effect of practice on ability, with shared-environment controls.
- **Survived refutation.** Skeptic: "n is large but it's self-reported practice and one cohort." Fair —
  that's exactly why this is MEDIUM, not HIGH, and why the next deep pass should pull the 2024 twin
  follow-ups. → **Stance kept, confidence explicitly capped at MEDIUM.**
