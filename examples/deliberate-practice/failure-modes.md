**Abstract.** The expensive mistakes — what looks like practice but doesn't build skill, and the errors a
real expert is defined by *not* making. Each is the inverse of a [[principles]] finding, cited the same way.

---

### F1. Counting hours instead of criterion-referenced progress
Treating "hours logged" as the scoreboard. The hours-to-mastery spread is an order of magnitude
([[gobet-campitelli-2007]]) and the 10,000 figure is a disowned average ([[ericsson-10000-hour-disavowal]]),
so an hour-count target measures the wrong thing. **Tell:** your goals are durations, not demonstrable
abilities. **Fix:** [[playbook]] T5.

### F2. Naive repetition — re-performing the whole skill, comfortably
Running the full skill end-to-end at your current level feels productive and isn't deliberate practice:
no edge-of-ability stretch, no isolated error-focus ([[ericsson-krampe-teschromer-1993]]). **Tell:** you
rarely fail during practice. **Fix:** [[playbook]] T3 — drill the weakest sub-component until you're
failing ~15–30%.

### F3. Volume without a feedback loop
Logging reps with no immediate, specific signal of right-vs-wrong. Volume without correction plateaus —
the mechanism behind necessary-but-not-sufficient ([[principles]] §2). **Tell:** you can't say what you
got wrong in your last session. **Fix:** [[playbook]] T2.

### F4. Ignoring domain structure
Assuming reps pay off equally everywhere. In ill-structured domains (management, "professions") practice
explains <1–4% of variance ([[macnamara-hambrick-oswald-2014]]); grinding there is low-ROI vs. stacking
other levers. **Tell:** you're drilling a domain whose rules shift faster than you can pattern-match them.
**Fix:** [[playbook]] T1.

### F5. Believing the 10,000-hour threshold
The threshold demotivates in both directions: fast learners expect to grind 10k and quit early; slow ones
pass 10k without mastery and conclude they "can't." It's a disowned myth ([[disagreements]] D2). **Tell:**
you're discouraged by an hour count. **Fix:** criterion-referenced goals, [[playbook]] T5.

### F6. Comparing your rate to the top of the distribution
Trainability and practice-propensity vary genetically ([[mosing-2014]]); benchmarking your improvement
rate against elite outliers ignores that. **Tell:** you quit because you're slower than the best, not
slower than *your own* prior curve. **Fix:** [[playbook]] T6. *(Failure mode of fatalism is the inverse
error — genetics sets rates, not ceilings you can locate in advance.)*

### F7. Conflating "practice" with "deliberate practice"
The most common analytical error, not just a learner error: treating all time-on-task as deliberate
practice. It inflates the apparent dose and muddies every effect-size estimate ([[principles]] §1;
the definitional crux of [[disagreements]] D1). **Tell:** you count playing/performing as practice.
