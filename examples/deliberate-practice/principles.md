## 1. What "deliberate practice" actually is
Ericsson's original construct is narrow: **effortful, goal-directed activity, designed (usually by a
teacher) to improve a specific aspect of performance, with immediate feedback and repetition at the edge
of current ability.** It is *not* mere experience, play, or time-on-task ([[ericsson-krampe-teschromer-1993]];
restated in [[ericsson-pool-peak-2016]]). The teacher-designed criterion is what separates *deliberate*
from merely *purposeful* practice — see [[glossary]].

`misconceptions:` "Practice = deliberate practice." Most accumulated hours in any domain are *not*
deliberate practice by Ericsson's criteria; conflating the two is the single most common error in the
popular literature — and, per Ericsson, in the skeptical meta-analyses too. That definitional dispute is
adjudicated in [[disagreements]] D1.

## 2. Deliberate practice is necessary but not sufficient
Across domains you essentially cannot reach elite performance *without* large accumulated practice — but
large practice does **not** guarantee it. In chess, the estimated minimum to reach master is ~3,000
hours, yet the practice required to reach a given level varies by roughly an order of magnitude across
players, and **some who practiced enormously never became masters** ([[gobet-campitelli-2007]];
[[hambrick-2014]]). That order-of-magnitude spread is the cleanest single refutation of any fixed-hours
"rule."

`misconceptions:` "Enough hours guarantees mastery." The heavily-practiced non-masters falsify this directly.

## 3. The size of the effect: a large minority, not the whole
The best meta-analysis (88 studies) found accumulated deliberate practice explains roughly **26% of
variance in games, 21% in music, 18% in sports, ~4% in education, and <1% in the professions**
([[macnamara-hambrick-oswald-2014]]; figures in [[benchmarks]]). Reading: practice matters *most* in
well-structured, stable-rule domains and *least* in ill-structured, unpredictable ones. `[inference]`
This domain-gradient is itself the actionable insight — it predicts *where* grinding reps pays (see
[[playbook]] T1).

`misconceptions:` "Practice explains most of expert performance." Even on the practice-favoring side of
the debate, the measured ceiling is ~a quarter of the variance in the friendliest domains.

## 4. The 10,000-hour "rule" is a myth — and its author says so
The figure is the *average* of the top violinist group in [[ericsson-krampe-teschromer-1993]]. It was
popularized as a threshold by Gladwell's *Outliers* ([[gladwell-outliers-2008]]). Ericsson publicly
rejects the interpretation: the number is "arbitrary," it was an average (about **half** the top group
had *not* reached 10,000 hours at the measured age), competition winners look more like ~25,000 hours,
and nothing magic happens at any hour count ([[ericsson-10000-hour-disavowal]]; [[ericsson-pool-peak-2016]]).

`misconceptions:` "10,000 hours makes you an expert." False on three counts: not a threshold, not
constant across domains, and not endorsed by the underlying researcher.

## 5. Genetics reaches the practice itself (the uncomfortable finding)
A 10,500-twin study found music practice is **40–70% heritable**, and the correlation between practice
and ability was **largely genetically mediated** — twins who practiced more were not reliably better than
their genetically identical co-twins who practiced less ([[mosing-2014]]). `[inference]` This breaks the
clean "practice → skill" causal arrow: propensity-to-practice and trainability may share genetic roots,
so naive practice–performance correlations overstate the causal effect of practice. Magnitude is held at
MEDIUM confidence (see [[disagreements]] D3).

`misconceptions:` "How much you practice is purely a choice/environment." Inclination to practice is
itself partly heritable.

## 6. Starting age and the developmental window
Earlier starters reach higher levels at equal practice hours; in chess, masters started younger than
equally-practiced experts ([[gobet-campitelli-2007]]). `[inference]` Age effects plausibly reflect a mix
of neural plasticity and more cumulative low-intensity exposure — the studies here don't cleanly separate
the two, so treat the *mechanism* as open (a [[frontier]] item).

`misconceptions:` "It's never too late and age doesn't matter." Age is a real, measured moderator, even
if its mechanism is unsettled.

## 7. Quality dominates quantity — but is under-measured `[convention]`
Practitioner consensus (and Ericsson's own framing in [[ericsson-pool-peak-2016]]) holds that *how* you
practice — edge-of-ability difficulty, tight feedback loops, error-focused repetition, building better
mental representations — matters more per hour than raw volume. **This is the thinnest-evidence pillar in
this KB** (see [[_index]] dashboard and [[frontier]]): mechanistically plausible and widely taught, but
the meta-analyses measured *quantity*, not quality, so the quality claim rests largely on convention +
theory, not on the effect sizes above.

`misconceptions:` "More hours is the lever." The lever is contested, but the strongest theory says it's
the *structure* of the hour, not the count — held with low confidence here pending a quality-focused pass
([[disagreements]] D4).
