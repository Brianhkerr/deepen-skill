# Experts — track-record-weighted source map

Ranked by the **proxy ladder**, not fame: (1) checkable past calls, (2) shows work / reproducible,
(3) calibration behavior, (4) peer recognition [weak], (0) fame alone [not evidence]. Rung that earned
the weight is recorded for each.

---

## Canon (foundational)

### K. Anders Ericsson (d. 2020) — credibility: **HIGH**, with a caveat
- **Who:** Originator of the deliberate-practice framework ([1993](https://eric.ed.gov/?id=EJ471947));
  author of *Peak* (2016).
- **Rung earned:** 2 (shows work — built the construct from primary data) + 3 (calibration — publicly
  corrected the 10,000-hour misreading rather than ride the fame).
- **Caveat / `[inference]`:** as the construct's author he has a stake in maximizing its explanatory
  share, and his strongest claims (practice as near-sole driver, "no innate limits") are the ones the
  data has pushed back on hardest. Weight his *definition* highly; weight his *effect-size optimism* lower.

### Malcolm Gladwell — credibility: **LOW** (as a science source)
- **Who:** Popularized "10,000 hours" in *Outliers*.
- **Rung earned:** 0 (fame/reach only). The underlying researcher disowns his interpretation
  ([Smithsonian](https://www.smithsonianmag.com/smart-news/10000-hour-rule-not-real-180952410/)).
  Listed because he's the source of the field's dominant *misconception* — useful to name, not to trust.

## Frontier (currently pushing the question)

### Brooke Macnamara — credibility: **HIGH**
- **Who:** Lead author of the field-defining skeptical meta-analysis
  ([2014](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/abs/10.1177/0956797614535810)) and the pre-registered,
  double-blind 2019 revisit of Ericsson's original
  ([Royal Society Open Science](https://royalsocietypublishing.org/doi/10.1098/rsos.190327)).
- **Rung earned:** 2 + 3 — pre-registration, double-blind procedure, and willingness to test her own
  prior with stronger methods than the original. This is the strongest methodological track record in
  the dispute. `[inference]`

### David Z. Hambrick — credibility: **HIGH**
- **Who:** Multifactorial-model proponent; co-author on the meta-analysis and chess analyses
  ([Hambrick et al., 2014, *Intelligence*](https://gwern.net/doc/psychology/chess/2014-hambrick.pdf)).
- **Rung earned:** 2 — argues from converging datasets (chess, music) rather than a single study.

### Fernand Gobet & Guillermo Campitelli — credibility: **HIGH** (chess specifically)
- **Who:** Longitudinal chess-practice data showing the huge hours-to-mastery spread
  ([2007](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S104160800700129X)).
- **Rung earned:** 1 + 2 — their specific, checkable claim (some >20k-hour players never master) has
  held up and is widely replicated in citation.

### Miriam Mosing et al. — credibility: **MEDIUM-HIGH**
- **Who:** Behavioral-genetics twin study on practice and music ability
  ([2014, *Psychological Science*](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/25079217/)).
- **Rung earned:** 2 — large genetically-informative sample (n≈10,500). Held at MEDIUM-HIGH not HIGH
  because it's a single flagship design on one domain; `[inference]` I'd want the 2024 follow-up twin
  work before treating the gene-mediation magnitude as settled.

## Loud-but-unsubstantiated consensus to flag
The "anyone can master anything with 10,000 hours" self-help genre. High reach, **rung 0**, contradicted
by the primary researcher and the variance data. Naming it is part of the value.
