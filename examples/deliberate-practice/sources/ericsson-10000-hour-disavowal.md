**Abstract.** The accessible, citable record that the "10,000-hour rule" is a journalistic distortion
the originating researcher rejects. Useful precisely because it pins the disavowal to Ericsson himself.
This anchors [[disagreements]] D2 and [[principles]] §4. **Fidelity note:** article fetched and read in
full; the Ericsson quotes are as reported by Smithsonian (and corroborated by 2012 BBC Future coverage),
not pulled from a primary Ericsson text.

## Substantive claims (the source's own argument)
1. **Provenance of the "rule."** The 10,000-hour figure was first proposed by Ericsson's group and made
   famous by Malcolm Gladwell's *Outliers* ([[gladwell-outliers-2008]]), which framed it as a threshold:
   exceptional expertise "requires at least 10,000 hours of practice."
2. **Ericsson's correction — it's an average, not a total or a threshold.** "There is nothing magical
   about the 10,000 figure… the best group of musicians had accumulated an **average, not a total**, of
   over 10,000 hours by the age of twenty." ^average-not-threshold The threshold reading is the error.
3. **The real spread is much higher and varies.** Winners of international music competitions look more
   like **~25,000 hours** of dedicated solitary practice ("three hours a day for more than 20 years") —
   not 10,000. The number is not a constant.
4. **Only deliberate practice counts toward the figure.** Ericsson stresses it must be "deliberate,
   dedicated time spent focusing on improvement," not mere experience — reinforcing the narrow construct
   from [[ericsson-krampe-teschromer-1993]].
5. **Context — it converges with the meta-analytic skeptics.** The article frames this alongside
   [[macnamara-hambrick-oswald-2014]] (reported there as practice explaining ~12% of variance on
   average), i.e. multiple independent lines say the popular rule is false.
