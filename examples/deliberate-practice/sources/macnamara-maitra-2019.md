**Abstract.** A pre-registered, better-controlled re-run of Ericsson's original 1993 violinist study —
the single cleanest test of the founding claim. It did **not** replicate the core finding, and the
narrow teacher-designed construct did not rescue the effect. This is the linchpin of why the KB's D1
stance survives Ericsson's strongest counter-objection. **Fidelity note:** the findings below are quoted
verbatim from the official Royal Society abstract and corroborated by the Purdue lab's hosted PDF.

## Substantive claims (the source's own argument)
1. **Design.** A pre-registered re-examination of Ericsson, Krampe & Tesch-Römer (1993)
   ([[ericsson-krampe-teschromer-1993]]) using violinists, with the methodological controls the original
   lacked. Open data deposited on Dryad.
2. **Primary result — failed replication.** "We did not replicate the core finding that accumulated
   amounts of deliberate practice corresponded to each skill level." ^failed-core-replication-2019 The
   monotonic best > good > teacher ordering of practice hours did not hold up.
3. **Effect size shrank.** "Overall, the size of the effect was substantial, but considerably smaller
   than the original study's effect size." So practice still matters — but less than the 1993 paper
   reported.
4. **The narrow construct didn't save it.** "Teacher-designed practice was perceived as less relevant to
   improving performance on the violin than practice alone," and "amount of teacher-designed practice
   did not account for more variance in performance than amount of practice alone." This directly answers
   Ericsson's "you measured the wrong construct" objection — isolating his narrow construct did **not**
   recover a larger effect.
5. **Implication.** Read together with [[macnamara-hambrick-oswald-2014]], the best-controlled evidence
   points the same way: deliberate practice is necessary but explains a minority of the variance, and the
   definitional fix Ericsson proposes doesn't reverse that.
