**Abstract.** The "is that all it takes?" paper: reanalyzes existing chess and music datasets and argues
deliberate practice, while important, leaves a large share of expert-performance variance to other
factors. Converging evidence for the multifactorial model behind [[disagreements]] D1. **Fidelity note:**
the thesis and the reanalyzed datasets are confirmed via the journal record and the same author team's
adjacent 2014 meta-analysis ([[macnamara-hambrick-oswald-2014]]); the paywalled full text was not fetched.

## Substantive claims (the source's own argument)
1. **Reanalysis, not a new dataset.** Re-examines published chess and music data (including
   [[gobet-campitelli-2007]]) to estimate how much accumulated deliberate practice actually explains.
2. **Large residual variance.** In these reanalyses, accumulated practice accounted for roughly **a
   third or less** of the variance in expert performance — important, but far from "all it takes." [the
   exact figures vary by dataset; reported here as the paper's qualitative conclusion, not a single
   re-derived number]
3. **Multifactorial model.** Expertise is the product of practice **plus** other contributors —
   general cognitive ability, working memory, starting age, and possibly genetics. This is the framing
   [[experts]] credits Hambrick for: arguing from *converging* datasets rather than one study.
4. **Same direction as the meta-analysis and twin work.** Slots alongside [[macnamara-hambrick-oswald-2014]]
   and [[mosing-2014]] as an independent line pointing to "necessary but not sufficient."
