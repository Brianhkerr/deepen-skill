**Abstract.** The book that turned Ericsson's group-average into a cultural "rule." It belongs in the
corpus only as the documented origin of the field's dominant misconception — named, not trusted.
**Fidelity note:** the book itself was not fetched; this digest is limited to its well-documented central
claim and its relationship to the primary literature, which is what the KB actually uses it for.

## Substantive claims (the source's argument — as it bears on this KB)
1. **The "10,000-hour rule."** Popularized the idea that ~10,000 hours of practice is what separates the
   best (the Beatles, Bill Gates) from everyone else — framed as a **threshold** anyone can cross with
   enough time. This is the framing the rest of the KB exists to correct.
2. **Why it's rung 0.** It is a journalistic synthesis, not research. The originating researcher (Ericsson)
   publicly rejects the threshold reading ([[ericsson-10000-hour-disavowal]]), and the variance data
   ([[macnamara-hambrick-oswald-2014]]) and chess spread ([[gobet-campitelli-2007]]) both falsify a fixed
   hour-count. See [[experts]] — listed under "loud-but-unsubstantiated consensus to flag."
3. **Its real value here.** Naming the precise origin of a misconception is part of the KB's job: it lets
   a reader trace *why* "10,000 hours" feels true (cultural saturation) versus whether it *is* true (no).

- Any empirical claim. Use it only as the citation for "where the 10k threshold framing came from."
