**Abstract.** The field-defining skeptical meta-analysis. Pooled the deliberate-practice literature
across every major domain and found practice explains a large *minority* of performance variance — not
the majority Ericsson's framing implies. This is the empirical anchor for the KB's headline stance
([[disagreements]] D1). **Fidelity note:** the load-bearing per-domain percentages are quoted
identically across five independent mirrors of the paper, so they're verified to the figure; the full
results tables were not re-derived from raw data.

## Substantive claims (the source's own argument)
1. **Scope.** Meta-analysis of 88 studies (157 effect sizes, total sample > 11,000), spanning music,
   games, sports, education, and professions. The largest such survey to date at publication.
2. **Headline result — variance explained by deliberate practice, by domain:** ^variance-by-domain-2014
   - **Games: 26%**
   - **Music: 21%**
   - **Sports: 18%**
   - **Education: ~4%**
   - **Professions: < 1%**
   These figures are the most-cited numbers in the whole debate. (Cross-domain average ≈ 12%, per the
   contemporaneous [[ericsson-10000-hour-disavowal]] coverage.)
3. **Interpretation — the domain gradient.** Practice matters *most* in well-structured, stable-rule
   domains (games) and *least* in ill-structured, unpredictable ones (professions). [inference, but the
   authors draw the same gradient] This gradient is itself the actionable insight: it predicts *where*
   grinding reps pays off — the basis of [[playbook]] T1.
4. **The core conclusion.** Deliberate practice is "important, but not as important as has been argued":
   it is necessary but **not sufficient**, and accounts for well under half the variance in every domain
   studied. Other factors (starting age, working memory, genetics) carry the rest.
5. **Method stance.** The analysis used the *broad* practice measures available in the literature — which
   is exactly the opening Ericsson's camp attacks (see "contested" below).

## Contested / how the other camp answers
- Ericsson's rebuttal: the meta-analysis aggregated studies that used a **broad** notion of practice, not
  his **narrow** teacher-designed construct, so it understates the true effect. A real, sourced objection.
- Why it doesn't overturn the headline (see [[disagreements]] D1 survived-refutation): the authors'
  own pre-registered follow-up [[macnamara-maitra-2019]] *isolated* teacher-designed practice and still
  found a smaller effect than the 1993 original — wounding the precise % but not the
  necessary-not-sufficient conclusion.
